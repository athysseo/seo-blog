What Is Technical SEO?

Technical SEO is the process of ensuring that a website meets the technical requirements of modern search engines. It focuses on improving the infrastructure of a site — how it is built, crawled and indexed — rather than its content.

Without solid technical foundations, even the best content can struggle to rank. A page that search engines cannot properly crawl will never appear in results, regardless of how well-written or relevant it is.

How Search Engines Crawl & Index Pages

Search engines use automated programs called crawlers (or spiders) to discover pages across the web. The crawler follows links from one page to the next, collecting information about each URL it visits.

Once a page is crawled, the search engine analyses its content and stores it in a vast database called the index. When a user performs a search, the engine retrieves relevant results from this index — not from the live web.

Key Elements of Technical SEO

XML Sitemap

An XML sitemap lists every important page on your website. Submit yours to Google Search Console to ensure all key pages are crawled promptly.

Robots.txt

The robots.txt file tells crawlers which pages they can and cannot access, freeing up crawl budget for your most important content.

Page Speed

Google uses page speed as a direct ranking factor. Use Google PageSpeed Insights to measure your scores and identify improvements.

Mobile-Friendliness

Google uses mobile-first indexing. Your site must be fully responsive and easy to use on a small screen.

HTTPS

Secure sites using HTTPS are given a ranking boost and trusted by browsers. Sites without it show a "Not Secure" warning.

Canonical Tags

Use a canonical tag (<link rel="canonical">) to prevent duplicate content issues across multiple URLs.

Where to Start

Start with a technical audit. Use Screaming Frog SEO Spider (free up to 500 URLs) to identify broken links, missing meta descriptions, duplicate titles, slow pages and redirect chains. Fix critical issues first, then work systematically.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about technical SEO, answered clearly.

What is the difference between crawling and indexing?+

Crawling is the discovery process — a search engine bot visits your page and reads its content and links. Indexing is what happens next: the search engine stores and organises that page in its database so it can appear in results.

A page can be crawled but not indexed (if it has a noindex tag), but a page cannot be indexed without first being crawled.

How do I check if Google has indexed my pages?+

The most reliable way is Google Search Console. Under the "Pages" report, you can see which URLs are indexed, which are excluded, and the reason for any exclusions.

You can also type site:yourdomain.com into Google to see a rough count of indexed pages, or use the URL Inspection tool in Search Console to check a specific page.

What is crawl budget and why does it matter?+

Crawl budget is the number of pages Google will crawl on your site within a given timeframe. For most small to medium sites, crawl budget is not a concern.

It becomes important on large sites with thousands of pages, where you want Googlebot spending its time on your most valuable content — not low-quality URLs or duplicate pages.

How long does it take Google to index a new page?+

Indexing time varies significantly. A new page on an established site can be indexed within a few hours. On a newer site, it can take days or weeks.

To speed things up: submit your sitemap to Search Console, use the URL Inspection tool to request indexing, and ensure the new page is linked from other pages on your site.

Do I need a sitemap if my site is small?+

A sitemap is not strictly required for small, well-linked sites, but it is still recommended as good practice. It gives you direct control over which pages you want Google to discover.

Sitemaps become especially valuable when you launch new content or have pages with few internal links pointing to them.

Can technical SEO issues cause a site to lose rankings suddenly?+

Yes. Common causes of sudden ranking drops include accidentally blocking crawlers via robots.txt, adding a noindex tag to important pages, a lapsed SSL certificate, or a server outage preventing Googlebot from accessing your site.

Regular technical audits and monitoring via Google Search Console can catch these issues early.

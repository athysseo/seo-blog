On-Page SEO

Complete On-Page SEO Checklist

Published May 2026 4 min read

What Is On-Page SEO?

On-page SEO refers to all the optimisations you make directly on a webpage to improve its visibility in search results. Unlike link building (which happens off your site), on-page SEO is entirely within your control.

Done well, it helps search engines understand exactly what a page is about — and signals to users that your content is relevant, trustworthy and worth clicking.

Title Tags

The title tag is the most important on-page element. It appears as the blue clickable headline in search results and tells both users and search engines what the page covers.

  • Include your primary keyword near the beginning
  • Keep it under 60 characters to avoid truncation in search results
  • Write for clicks — make it compelling, not just keyword-stuffed
  • Every page should have a unique title tag

Meta Descriptions

Meta descriptions do not directly influence rankings, but they do affect click-through rates — which matters for organic traffic volume.

  • Keep them under 160 characters
  • Include your primary keyword naturally
  • Write a clear, benefit-led summary of what the page delivers
  • Treat it like ad copy — it should earn the click

Headings (H1, H2, H3)

Headings create structure that helps both readers and search engines understand how a page is organised.

  • Use exactly one H1 per page — it should match or closely reflect the title tag
  • Use H2 tags for main sections and H3 for sub-sections
  • Include related keywords in headings naturally — never force them

Images

Images improve engagement but can hurt performance and rankings if not handled correctly.

  • Add descriptive alt text to every image — this helps search engines understand the image and improves accessibility
  • Compress images before uploading (use tools like Squoosh or ShortPixel)
  • Use modern formats like WebP where possible
  • Set explicit width and height attributes to prevent layout shift

Internal Linking

Internal links connect your pages together, distribute ranking authority and help users navigate to related content.

  • Link to related articles and key pages naturally within the body copy
  • Use descriptive anchor text — avoid "click here" or "read more"
  • Ensure every important page has at least a few internal links pointing to it

URL Structure

A clean, descriptive URL helps both users and search engines understand what a page is about before visiting it.

  • Keep URLs short and descriptive: /on-page-seo-checklist
  • Use hyphens to separate words, not underscores
  • Include the primary keyword where it reads naturally
  • Avoid dynamic parameters in URLs where possible

Content Quality

Google's ranking systems are designed to reward content that demonstrates experience, expertise, authoritativeness and trustworthiness (E-E-A-T). Before publishing, ask yourself:

  • Does this page fully answer the reader's question?
  • Is it more thorough or more accurate than what currently ranks?
  • Is it easy to read — short paragraphs, clear headings, no fluff?

Frequently Asked Questions

Does keyword density still matter for on-page SEO?
No — keyword stuffing is harmful. Instead, focus on semantic relevance and natural language. Use related terms (LSI keywords) and cover the topic comprehensively. Google understands context without exact‑match repetition.
How long should my content be for good on-page SEO?
There is no ideal length. The best length fully satisfies the user’s intent. For informational queries, 1500‑2500 words often perform well, but a 500‑word page that directly answers a question can also rank. Prioritise completeness over word count.
Should I include the target keyword in the H1 and title tag?
Yes — the title tag and H1 should both contain your primary keyword (ideally near the start) and be similar but not identical. The title tag targets search result clicks, while the H1 sets the page’s main topic for users.
What’s the difference between nofollow and internal links?
Nofollow links (rel="nofollow") tell search engines not to pass ranking credit to the linked page. For internal linking, you normally do NOT use nofollow — you want authority to flow. Reserve nofollow for user‑generated content or paid links.
